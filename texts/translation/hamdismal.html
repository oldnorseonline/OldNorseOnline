1. Sprouted on the path

sorrows, tears, 

elves’ tears

glee-steaming; 

early in the morning

of men’s misfortunes

each/every sorrow

kindled by grief


  1. It was not 

now nor yesterday,

it has been a long

time since, 

few are so ancient 

(to be) half so far (back) as this, 

when Guðrún

born of Gjuka

incited her young sons

to avenge Svanhildr. 


  1. “Your sister was

called Svanhildr,

whom Jörmunrekkr

trampled with horses

white and black,

on the war paths,

with the spiteful hoof-tread

of the Goth’s horses.


  1. You are pushed back, 

great kings,

the only living thread

of my ancestry. 


  1. I am stood alone

like the aspen in the forest, 

with few kinsmen

like a fir to a branch

stripped of joy

like wood to the leaves, 

when the twig-scather

comes on a warm day.”


  1. Then said Hamðir

the high-minded: 

“Little then, Guðrún, might you 

praise Högna’s courage, 

when they awakened

Sigurðr from sleep, 

you were sat in the bed

and the killers laughed (ridiculed you). 


  1. Your embroidered coverlets

in blue and white

woven by Waylands,

floated in your husband’s blood; 

then Sigurðr died, 

you sat over the dead, 

you heeded no joy, 

Gunnarr willed it thus for you. 


  1. You thought to afflict Atla

with Erpr’s murder

and the slaying of Eitil, 

but it was yet worse for you; 

so must each (man) from (all) others

defend himself to the death

with sore-biting sword

that afflicts him not. 


  1. Then Sörli said thus 

— he had good sense: 

“I will not argue

with my mother; 

each of you thinks

there is more to be said. 

What ask you now, Guðrún, 

which you weep for a want of?


  1. You weep for your brothers

and (your) sweet sons

laid down in battle; 

we-two shall you also, Guðrún, 

weep for both,

who sit her, (ill) fated on our horses,

we will die far away.”


  1. They went out the courtyard, 

building their roar, 

then the youths journeyed

over wet mountains

to avenge murder. 


  1. Then Erpr spoke,

only once,

sporting across the land

on horseback:

It’s no good to show the way 

to cowards.”

They said the bastard

was very bold. 


  1. They met on the street

the very sly one: 

“How might the weakling

help us?”


  1. The other-mothered one answered, 

said thus that he would

help the kinsmen

like one foot to another. 

“How can a foot

to a foot help, 

or a body’s 

one hand the other?”


  1. They drew from the sheath

sheath-iron,

the swords’ edges

of the ogress’s delight; 

they cut their strength

by a third,

sent the young man

to fall to the earth. 


  1. (Then) shook their cloaks, 

fastened their swords,

and the god-born

pulled on their good velvet. 


  1. Further on their way

they found the woeful path

and their sister’s son

wounded, maimed on

the wind-cold worrying-tree (gallows)

west of the fortress, 

the crane-bait murmured (rustled?) endlessly, 

it wasn’t pleasant to linger


  1. Glee was in the hall

men rejoiced in ale,

and did not hear

the horses

until a courageous (lit. courage-full) man

howled through his horn.


  1. They went to tell

Jörmunrekkr

that men under helmets

were sighted:

“Be advised by your counsel, 

nobles are coming; 

in memory of the mighty

men’s

sister you trampled.”


  1. Then Jörmunrekkr laughed, 

(his) hand raked through his whiskers, 

brought himself to the bloodthirst,

sloshing to fighting spirit with wine; 

shook his brown locks, 

looked upon his white shield, 

let the gold goblet

roll in his hand. 


  1. “I would think myself glad, 

if I could see

Hamðr and Sörli

in my hall; 

then I would bind the boys

with bow-strings, 

Gjúki’s well-born boys

fastened on the gallows.”


  1. Then spoke the glad Hróðr,

stood by the doors,

slim-fingered, (and) said

to those men: 

“For what they promise

would not be possible,

could two men alone

bind or beat

ten hundred Goths

in battle in the fortress?”


  1. Strife reached the hall

ale-cups splashed

warriors lay in blood

from the Goth’s breasts.


  1. Then Hamðir the high minded

said this: 

“You asked, Jörmunrekkr,

for us to come, 

brothers of the same mother,

into your fort. 

You see your own feet,

you see your own hands, 

Jörmunrekkr, flung

into the hot fire.”


  1. Then roared

the almighty king

bold in armour

as a bear growls:

“Stone the men

whom all steel does not bite,

nor blades, nor iron,

Jónakr’s sons”. 


  1. Then Hamðir the high minded

said this:

“You won misfortune, brother

when you opened that bag; 

often from this bag

poor advice comes.”


  1. “You would have a heart, Hamðr,

if you had good sense,

a man wanting in mother’s wisdom

is wanting much.”


  1. “Off now would be the head

if Erpr lived, 

our brother, the battle-valiant,

whom we two slayed on the way, 

a battle-brave man,

spurred by the díses,

the man built-for-battle,

I was driven to kill.


  1. I think we will not

resort to the wolves’ example,

to scrap at each other

like the Norna’s hounds,

who, begotten in destruction,

are greedy.


  1. Well have we fought,

standing on slain goths

over battle-corpses

like eagles on branches; 

we have gained good glory,

although we should now or yesterday die, 

no man lives, the evening

after the norna speak.”


  1. There Sörli fell

at the hall’s gable,

and Hamðir sank

at the back of the hall.


This is called the ancient lay of Hamðir.
